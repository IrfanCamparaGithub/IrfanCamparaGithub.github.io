Title: How has Food Imports and Shipping Changed In Recent Years and Why Does it Contribute to Your Plate?

Audience & Intent:

This food import tale is designed for essentially everyone within the world. While that may seem generic, food import and international logistics impacts the lives of everyone whether you know it or not. Food imports can make or break diet culture, pricing and the overall wellness of individuals within a country. While there are many different individuals who may be interested in reading such a report, the key audience members would be international economics who may be focused on food logistics, farmers across the globe who want to see what the U.S is importing in most and American citizens who want to see which foods are projected to increase, decrease our maintain their current price and country of origin. The main goal is that these users are able to get a sense of which countries the U.S relies on the most for major food imports, how important food categories and importing countries have shifted over time, how the U.S food prices impact the world and vice versa, and how current and past political policies have shifted food import specifics such as importing countries, price and highest importing volume.

Inflation, Inflation, Inflation, How Food Prices Have Suffered From This Dreaded Term:

Inflation is a common culprit of many issues that Americans and citizens from across the world have to deal with, and will continue to deal with for the rest of eternity. Below is a playable line chart where you can view all categories as a whole or hyper focus on a specific food group.

While we yet again will not delve into major details, we will explain some of the specific major changes and the respective reasoning behind such changes.

2008: A major downward spike is seen. The 2008 financial crisis, which was caused by synthetic securities banks created that were backed by poor quality mortgages, finally broke and caused the entire global economy to melt. This caused the global economy to slow and deflation for almost every single item to ensue.

2011: In contrast to 2008, a major spike in almost every single food category was seen. This spike can mainly be attributed to extreme weather events, biofuel booms and the weakness of the U.S dollar. A combination of these three macroeconomic headwinds resulted in a major spike for food imports and likely caused many Americans to have much less food on their plates.

2020: Another sharp spike is seen in 2020 and this is mainly attributed to the COVID-19 pandemic, which led to significant money printing and reductions in interest rates to ensure the global economy would not crash. As such, food import prices increased dramatically.

2024: While most food groups maintained stability, cocoa and chocolate saw a massive spike. The reasoning behind this is that global production for cocoa fell by a large margin and tariffs on major producers like Ghana and the Ivory Coast also led to their major export to spike in price as well.

While there are many more nuanced spikes and crashes, these are the main events we consider relevant and hope are a little better explained now.

Import Volume Changes Over Time

Now that we have a snapshot of the largest food imports by country, a map for the largest food groups and a moving price line chart, it is time to see how different categories have shifted in overall volume throughout time.

The main trend that we can see is that major food groups at the beginning of the century are mainly live meat animals, fruits and grains, and then live meat animals decline with fruits and vegetables leading the pack within recent years. The remaining food categories maintained relatively the same ordering throughout the turn of the century to now. This exploration has shown that the food on our plates depends deeply on logistics, economic and political systems that we often take for granted. The United States would not be a functioning country without having access to a diverse set of food groups from the partners that support it.
